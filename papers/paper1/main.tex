\begin{abstract}
We introduce a nerve protocol — discrete neuroletters drawn from a
learned local vocabulary, multiplexed on gamma/theta rhythms, and
routed over a sparse learned topology — that lets heterogeneous
neural modules (World Model Languages, or WMLs) exchange information
without sharing a substrate. We validate the protocol in two stages.
Track-P demonstrates protocol correctness on toy signals (Gate P);
Track-W demonstrates that MLP-based and LIF-based WMLs interoperate
with less than 5\% performance gap through the same nerve interface
(Gate W). A merge experiment fine-tunes only the per-edge transducers
and retains 95\% of baseline accuracy (Gate M). All experimental
numbers in this draft resolve to deterministic run identifiers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Related Work}

The nerve protocol sits at the intersection of four research lines. We relate each briefly below.

\textbf{Predictive coding.} Rao and Ballard~\cite{rao1999predictive} introduced predictive coding as a unifying interpretation of extra-classical receptive-field effects in the visual cortex, where each layer transmits its prediction downstream and receives a residual error upstream. Bastos et al.~\cite{bastos2012canonical} then proposed the canonical microcircuit for predictive coding, mapping predictions to $\gamma$-band activity in superficial layers and errors to $\theta$/$\alpha$-band activity in deep layers. Our $\gamma$/$\theta$ multiplexing (see \S\ref{sec:method}) is a direct computational transcription of this canonical microcircuit, with $\gamma$-priority replacing the biological phase-locking.

\textbf{Discrete latent codebooks.} van den Oord et al.~\cite{vandenoord2017neural} introduced VQ-VAE, the vector-quantized variational auto-encoder in which a continuous encoder output is snapped to one of $K$ discrete codes. We reuse the commitment-loss training recipe for each WML's local codebook (\S\ref{sec:method}). Zeghidour et al.~\cite{zeghidour2022soundstream} observed that random-init VQ training collapses onto a small subset of codes, and proposed codebook rotation --- re-seeding unused codes with live input points. Our Plan 4a experiments confirm that rotation is necessary for random-init convergence on the $64$-code alphabet (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).

\textbf{Surrogate-gradient spiking neural networks.} Neftci et al.~\cite{neftci2019surrogate} survey surrogate-gradient training of SNNs, where the non-differentiable Heaviside spike is replaced at back-propagation time by a smooth pseudo-derivative (we use the fast-sigmoid variant). This makes our \texttt{LifWML} implementation directly trainable alongside the differentiable \texttt{MlpWML} through the same task loss, which is the precondition for our polymorphie claim.

To our knowledge, no prior system combines all four threads into a single substrate-agnostic communication protocol. The nerve protocol contributes the integration, not any of the individual techniques.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Neuroletters}
A neuroletter is a tuple $(code, role, phase, src, dst, t)$ with
$code \in \{0, \dots, 63\}$, $role \in \{\pi, \varepsilon\}$,
$phase \in \{\gamma, \theta\}$, and $(src, dst)$ pointing at WMLs.
Each WML owns a local codebook of 64 embeddings; per-edge
transducers map between neighbours' codebooks.

\subsection{Sparse routing and priority}
The topology is a learned directed graph with $K \ll N$ active
out-edges per WML, sampled via Gumbel-softmax top-K during training
and frozen to $\{0, 1\}$ at inference. $\gamma$ has priority over
$\theta$ whenever both oscillators are active, ensuring a clean
multiplexing window without phase-locking the oscillators.

\subsection{WML polymorphism}
\texttt{MlpWML} implements the WML protocol via a 4-layer MLP and
a surprise-gated $\varepsilon$ head; \texttt{LifWML} implements the
same protocol via a population of LIF neurons with surrogate-gradient
spiking. Both expose only the \texttt{step(nerve, t)} entry point.

\subsection{Scaling Behaviour}
\label{sec:scaling}

To test whether the polymorphie and continual-learning claims survive
a larger pool, we repeat W1, W2 and W4 at $N = 16$ and $N = 32$ WMLs
(respectively 8 MLP + 8 LIF and 16 MLP + 16 LIF, interleaved).
Fan-out scales with $k(N) = \max(2, \lceil \log_2 N \rceil)$ so the
directed graph remains sparse ($k = 4$ at $N = 16$, $k = 5$ at $N = 32$).

\section{Threats to Validity}

Our experiments use synthetic tasks and small WML pools. Three threats deserve mention.

\textit{Task saturation and honest harder-task measurement.} \texttt{FlowProxyTask} 4-class is trivially linearly separable; both MLP and LIF pools converge to 1.0 on it. The 0\% polymorphie gap (Figure~\ref{fig:w2-histogram}) is therefore a degenerate best case. We implemented \texttt{HardFlowProxyTask} --- a 12-class variant where the class label XORs two hyperplane projections on the noise component, so a linear probe plateaus at $\sim 0.6$. On that task \texttt{run\_w2\_hard} measures $\text{acc}_{\text{MLP}} = 0.547$, $\text{acc}_{\text{LIF}} = 0.480$, giving a \textbf{polymorphie gap of $12.1\%$} --- well above our 5\% target. This is an honest negative result: LIF's current cosine pattern-match decoder lags the MLP $\pi$ head on non-linear tasks, so the substrate-agnostic claim in this paper applies to linearly-separable regimes. Closing the gap on harder tasks is explicit future work (see spec §13.1).

\textit{Scale.} All experiments use $N=4$ WMLs with $K=1$--2 fan-out. Whether the 5\% polymorphie gap holds at $N=16$ or $N=32$ is an open question tracked in a separate scaling plan; the router's sparsity and the $\gamma$-priority rule may interact non-trivially at scale.

\textit{Single-seed artefacts.} The Gate P and Gate M pilots in this draft run on a single seed. Where multi-seed data exists (W2, W4, P1), we report it; elsewhere the reader should treat numbers as suggestive rather than statistically validated.

\section{Reproducibility}

All measurements in this paper resolve to a deterministic \texttt{run\_id} via \texttt{harness/run\_registry.py}. The frozen golden NPZ artefacts in \texttt{tests/golden/} lock in bit-stability.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Gate P numbers} — \texttt{uv run python scripts/track\_p\_pilot.py} prints a JSON report with \texttt{all\_passed: true}.
  \item \textbf{Gate W numbers} — \texttt{uv run python scripts/track\_w\_pilot.py} prints the same style report covering W1--W4.
  \item \textbf{Gate M ratio} — \texttt{uv run python scripts/merge\_pilot.py} prints \texttt{acc\_merged / acc\_mock\_baseline $\geq$ 0.95}.
  \item \textbf{Ablation numbers} — \texttt{run\_p3\_no\_priority}, \texttt{run\_w2\_true\_lif}, \texttt{run\_w4\_rehearsal}, and \texttt{run\_p1\_random\_init} in the same scripts.
  \item \textbf{Figures} — \texttt{uv run python scripts/render\_paper\_figures.py} regenerates every figure from frozen golden data and multi-seed pilot output.
\end{itemize}

Every numeric claim has a backing test in \texttt{tests/integration/} or \texttt{tests/unit/}. Running \texttt{uv run pytest} reproduces all gate assertions in under a minute on commodity Apple Silicon.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
The experiments in this draft use toy signals and small WML pools
($N=4$). Scaling to larger pools, LLM-backed WMLs, and hardware
neuromorphic deployment is left for follow-up work. The $\gamma$
priority rule suffices in simulation; a phase-locked oscillator
regime would give the same invariant without the rule and is a
natural next step.
