\begin{abstract}
We introduce a nerve protocol --- discrete neuroletters
(\emph{glossary: tokens from a fixed 64-element alphabet}) drawn
from a learned local vocabulary, multiplexed on $\gamma / \theta$
rhythms (\emph{glossary: frequency bands from predictive-coding
biology associated with prediction and error signals, used here
as a structural correctness contract --- see \S~Method}), and
routed over a sparse learned topology --- that lets heterogeneous
neural modules (World Model Languages, or WMLs) \emph{exchange
information} without sharing a substrate. We validate two claims
empirically. \textbf{(A) Substrate-agnostic polymorphism.} Three
structurally distinct substrates --- stateless MLP, spiking LIF
with surrogate-gradient training, and attention-based Transformer ---
reach comparable task accuracies on a non-linear benchmark
(HardFlowProxyTask, XOR-on-noise 12-class). A four-point scaling law
traces the pairwise MLP / LIF gap from median $10.7\%$ at $N=2$
through $6.7\%$ at $N=16$, $2.4\%$ at $N=32$, plateau $2.7\%$ at
$N=64$; direction stable (LIF $\geq$ MLP) in 19/20 multi-seed
measurements (5 seeds $\times$ 4 $N$-points, with $N=2$ completed
in v1.7.0; 4/5 at $N=2$, 5/5 at $N{=}16$, $N{=}32$, $N{=}64$). The triple-substrate version closes from $8.2\%$ at
$N=15$ to $4.3\%$ at $N=60$ over 5 seeds each.
\textbf{(B) Substrate-agnostic information transmission.} For two
independently trained substrates on the same input, the mutual
information between their emitted codes over the 64-code alphabet
is $\mathrm{MI} / H = 0.91$ at $N=1$, strengthening to $0.96$ at
$N=16$ pool scale (192 cross-pair measurements). Round-trip fidelity
through learned transducers MLP $\to$ LIF $\to$ MLP retains $99\%$
of direct-MLP accuracy; cross-substrate merge (a frozen LIF decoding
MLP-emitted codes alone through a learned transducer) retains
$97\%$. The finding generalizes to a structurally independent
distribution (MoonsTask, $\mathrm{MI} / H = 0.74$). A post-hoc analysis via Huh et al.'s~\cite{huh2024platonic} mutual-kNN kernel on continuous pre-VQ embeddings yields cross-substrate alignment $0.174$ at $k{=}10$ ($18.8\times$ the random baseline, 3 seeds), situating nerve-wml as an empirical validation of the Platonic Representation Hypothesis on heterogeneous biologically-inspired substrates. All numeric claims
are reproducible via \texttt{uv run pytest} with explicit seeds;
code and figures of this v1.7.0 snapshot are archived on Zenodo
(concept DOI \href{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19656342}{10.5281/zenodo.19656342},
version DOI \href{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19666405}{10.5281/zenodo.19666405}),
and pre-registered on OSF as part of the dreamOfkiki research
programme (\href{https://doi.org/10.17605/OSF.IO/Q6JYN}{10.17605/OSF.IO/Q6JYN}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Related Work}

The nerve protocol sits at the intersection of four research lines. We relate each briefly below.

\textbf{Predictive coding.} Rao and Ballard~\cite{rao1999predictive} introduced predictive coding as a unifying interpretation of extra-classical receptive-field effects in the visual cortex, where each layer transmits its prediction downstream and receives a residual error upstream. Bastos et al.~\cite{bastos2012canonical} then proposed the canonical microcircuit for predictive coding, mapping predictions to $\gamma$-band activity in superficial layers and errors to $\theta$/$\alpha$-band activity in deep layers. Our $\gamma$/$\theta$ multiplexing (see \S\ref{sec:method}) is a direct computational transcription of this canonical microcircuit, with $\gamma$-priority replacing the biological phase-locking.

\textbf{Discrete latent codebooks.} van den Oord et al.~\cite{vandenoord2017neural} introduced VQ-VAE, the vector-quantized variational auto-encoder in which a continuous encoder output is snapped to one of $K$ discrete codes. We reuse the commitment-loss training recipe for each WML's local codebook (\S\ref{sec:method}). Zeghidour et al.~\cite{zeghidour2022soundstream} observed that random-init VQ training collapses onto a small subset of codes, and proposed codebook rotation --- re-seeding unused codes with live input points. Our Plan 4a experiments confirm that rotation is necessary for random-init convergence on the $64$-code alphabet (\S\ref{sec:experiments}). Liu et al.~\cite{liu2021dvnc} further demonstrated VQ bottlenecks as inter-module communication channels (DVNC), validating that discretisation improves compositional generalisation when neural modules recombine. More recently, Peng et al.~\cite{peng2025gridlikevq} introduce Grid-like Code Quantization to compress observation-action sequences in world models, drawing on grid-cell attractor dynamics as a biologically-motivated VQ prior. Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2025channelawarevq} propose channel-aware VQ for semantic communication on discrete channels, framing VQ as a joint source-channel coding problem. Our nerve-wml is orthogonal: we treat the codebook as a fixed protocol alphabet and measure the information-theoretic alignment of substrates emitting into this alphabet, rather than optimising the codebook for a specific communication channel or world-model prior.

\textbf{Surrogate-gradient spiking neural networks.} Neftci et al.~\cite{neftci2019surrogate} survey surrogate-gradient training of SNNs, where the non-differentiable Heaviside spike is replaced at back-propagation time by a smooth pseudo-derivative (we use the fast-sigmoid variant). This makes our \texttt{LifWML} implementation directly trainable alongside the differentiable \texttt{MlpWML} through the same task loss, which is the precondition for our polymorphie claim.

\textbf{Representation similarity and cross-architecture communication.} Kornblith et al.~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} introduced linear and RBF Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA), a Hilbert--Schmidt Independence Criterion-based metric that is invariant to orthogonal transformation and isotropic scaling of hidden representations. Morcos, Raghu, and Bengio~\cite{morcos2018insights} introduced Projection-Weighted CCA (PWCCA) on the same broad question. Both operate on \emph{continuous} hidden states. Moschella et al.~\cite{moschella2022relative} subsequently proposed \emph{relative representations}: encoding each sample as a vector of cosine similarities to a fixed anchor set, which achieves invariance to angle-preserving transformations and enables zero-shot model stitching across independently trained models (their own Table~5 reports, for example, a ViT$\to$ResNet50 stitch accuracy of $4.69\%$ absolute vs $84.46\%$ relative on CIFAR-100). Their Figure~7 mentions vector-quantised anchors as an alternative similarity function but explicitly leaves the discrete variant as future work. Saxe et al.~\cite{saxe2024universality} argue empirically for a representation-universality hypothesis across biological and artificial networks using representational dissimilarity analysis (Pearson correlation of RDMs), also on continuous representations. Huh et al.~\cite{huh2024platonic} generalise these findings into the \emph{Platonic Representation Hypothesis}: representations across architectures, modalities, and training objectives converge toward a shared statistical model of reality. Our mutual-kNN alignment measurement in \S\ref{sec:info-tx}~(6) situates nerve-wml as an empirical validation of PRH on \emph{structurally heterogeneous biologically-inspired substrates} --- ANN vs spiking LIF with surrogate-gradient --- rather than the vision/language cross-modality pairs that dominate their evidence. We use \emph{biologically-inspired} rather than \emph{biological} to distinguish surrogate-gradient LIF from actual neural recordings, consistent with~\cite{aristotelianprh2026}'s caution against rhetorical overextension of PRH. Our measured $0.174$ sits near the lower bound of Huh et al.'s cross-modality range ($0.15$--$0.25$), which extends up to $\sim 0.50$ for same-modality pairs; this placement reflects the structural heterogeneity between ANN and spiking substrates, not a ceiling on PRH validity.

Our \texttt{MI}/\texttt{H} ratio on emitted discrete codes (\S\ref{sec:info-tx}) occupies the slot this family leaves open: we measure, at the discrete protocol-output level, what Kornblith's CKA, Morcos's PWCCA, Moschella's relative representations, and Saxe's RDMs measure on continuous hidden states. Empirically we observe $\mathrm{MI}/\mathrm{H} \approx 0.953$ versus $\mathrm{CKA}$ on the corresponding argmax one-hots of $0.910$ (\S\ref{sec:info-tx}); the $4.3$-percentage-point gap tracks soft many-to-one code mappings that kernel-alignment metrics miss by construction.

\textbf{Knowledge distillation.} Hinton et al.~\cite{hinton2015distilling} proposed training a student network end-to-end by minimising the weighted loss $\alpha\,\mathrm{CE}(p_S(\cdot; T), p_T(\cdot; T))\,T^2 + (1-\alpha)\,\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{hard}}(y)$, where $T$ is a temperature that softens the teacher's class distribution. Our cross-substrate merge (Test (3), \S\ref{sec:info-tx}) is structurally different: both substrates are \emph{frozen}, only a linear transducer between their emission spaces is trained, supervision is ground-truth labels only, and the passed message is a logit vector over a $64$-code protocol alphabet rather than a softmax over $n_{\mathrm{classes}}$ label values. This separates protocol channel capacity from student learning capacity.

To our knowledge, no prior system combines all five threads into a single substrate-agnostic communication protocol with an explicit discrete-code measurement methodology. The nerve protocol contributes the integration, not any of the individual techniques.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Neuroletters}
A neuroletter is a tuple $(code, role, phase, src, dst, t)$ with
$code \in \{0, \dots, 63\}$, $role \in \{\pi, \varepsilon\}$,
$phase \in \{\gamma, \theta\}$, and $(src, dst)$ pointing at WMLs.
Each WML owns a local codebook of 64 embeddings; per-edge
transducers map between neighbours' codebooks.

\subsection{Sparse routing and priority}
The topology is a learned directed graph with $K \ll N$ active
out-edges per WML, sampled via Gumbel-softmax top-K during training
and frozen to $\{0, 1\}$ at inference. The $\gamma / \theta$ multiplex
invariant (N-3: $\text{role}{=}\varepsilon \Leftrightarrow
\text{phase}{=}\theta$) functions as a \emph{formal correctness
contract} --- a type-checker for letter well-formedness at
composition time --- rather than a differential computational
mechanism. In the experiments reported in this paper all substrates
emit $\pi$ letters in the $\gamma$ phase exclusively (\S Threats to
Validity), so the invariant is satisfied by construction; its value
is catching composition bugs when downstream modules inadvertently
emit malformed letters (\S\,(7) guard-injection test). The
Bastos-Friston 2012 cortical-microcircuit citation is preserved as
the structural inspiration, not as a claim of empirical mechanism.

\subsection{WML polymorphism}
\texttt{MlpWML} implements the WML protocol via a 4-layer MLP and
a surprise-gated $\varepsilon$ head; \texttt{LifWML} implements the
same protocol via a population of LIF neurons with surrogate-gradient
spiking. Both expose only the \texttt{step(nerve, t)} entry point.

\subsection{Matched-capacity design}
\label{sec:matched-capacity}
Throughout the paper we fix the substrate capacity to
$d_{\mathrm{hidden}} = n_{\mathrm{neurons}}$ (default $16$, with
sensitivity sweep at $\{8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256\}$ reported in
\S\,(11) and Sleep-EDF matched-scale runs). This \emph{under-parameterises}
the substrates by SOTA standards (e.g.\ SLA-MLP on Sleep-EDF uses
$\sim 10^6$ parameters and achieves $95.5\%$; our $d_{\mathrm{hidden}}
= 128$ configuration uses $\sim 10^4$ parameters and achieves
$\sim 80\%$). The choice is deliberate: fixing the parameter budget
equal between MLP and LIF isolates the \emph{architectural} difference
(dense ReLU vs spiking membrane dynamics) from capacity-driven
differences. Any observed substrate-intrinsic gap (the $2$--$3\%$
plateau of Claim A) is then attributable to substrate structure, not
to one architecture having more free parameters than the other. This
rigour comes at a cost: our absolute accuracies on real-world
benchmarks are below the unrestricted-capacity SOTA. We accept that
cost in exchange for apples-to-apples substrate comparability, and
explicitly flag it in the experiments that follow. The paper's claims
are about \emph{substrate-relative} information transmission, not
about maximum achievable task competence.

\section{Threats to Validity}

Our experiments use synthetic tasks and small WML pools. Three threats deserve mention.

\textit{Task saturation and honest harder-task measurement.} \texttt{FlowProxyTask} 4-class is trivially linearly separable; both MLP and LIF pools converge to 1.0 on it. The 0\% polymorphie gap (Figure~\ref{fig:w2-histogram}) is therefore a degenerate best case. We implemented \texttt{HardFlowProxyTask} --- a 12-class variant where the class label XORs two hyperplane projections on the noise component, so a linear probe plateaus at $\sim 0.6$.

\textbf{v0.3 measurement (cosine decoder).} With \texttt{LifWML}'s original fixed cosine-similarity decoder, \texttt{run\_w2\_hard} reported $\text{acc}_{\text{MLP}} = 0.547$, $\text{acc}_{\text{LIF}} = 0.480$, a polymorphie gap of $12.1\%$. We initially attributed this to a genuine substrate limitation.

\textbf{v0.4 update (symmetric heads).} A re-read of the code revealed that the observed gap was \emph{not} substrate-intrinsic but a crippled-readout artefact: \texttt{MlpWML} used a learned linear $\pi$ head while \texttt{LifWML} used a fixed cosine match against a randomly initialized codebook. After adding a symmetric \texttt{emit\_head\_pi = nn.Linear(n\_neurons, alphabet\_size)} to \texttt{LifWML} (protocol \texttt{step()} keeps the cosine pattern-match decoder intact, preserving the N-1 invariant), the same pilot now reports $\text{acc}_{\text{MLP}} = 0.547$, $\text{acc}_{\text{LIF}} = 0.611$ --- a $10.7\%$ gap in the \emph{opposite} direction. The spike + surrogate pipeline, once given a trainable readout, edges out the $16$-dim MLP core on XOR-on-noise.

\textbf{v0.5 update (multi-seed at scale).} Scaling the hard task to a $16$-WML pool (eight MLPs, eight LIFs) gave a \emph{single-seed} gap of $1.68\%$, which we initially read as statistical closure. Multi-seed re-measurement with $\text{seeds} \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}$ reveals the true N=16 distribution: $\text{mean} = 6.29\%$, $\text{median} = 6.71\%$, $\text{p25} = 4.67\%$, $\text{p75} = 8.02\%$, $\text{max} = 10.35\%$. The seed-0 estimate was lucky; the N=16 gap is distributionally $\sim 7\%$ --- above the $5\%$ contract.

\textbf{v0.6 update (scaling law at N=32).} Extending the multi-seed protocol to a $32$-WML pool (sixteen MLPs, sixteen LIFs) produces $\text{mean} = 3.00\%$, $\text{median} = 2.39\%$, $\text{p25} = 1.64\%$, $\text{p75} = 4.66\%$, $\text{max} = 4.75\%$. Every seed at N=32 falls under the $5\%$ contract. Together with the N=16 and N=2 references, this traces a three-point scaling law:

\textbf{v0.7 update (plateau at N=64).} The fourth scaling point rules out an asymptotic decay to zero: $\text{mean} = 2.50\%$, $\text{median} = 2.73\%$, $\text{p25} = 1.70\%$, $\text{p75} = 3.31\%$, $\text{max} = 3.71\%$. Comparing to $N=32$'s $2.39\%$ median, we observe a \emph{plateau} in the $\sim 2\text{--}3\%$ band rather than further monotonic decrease. Our honest reading, finalized across four scales and 16 independent runs: (a) on individual instances, spike expressivity adds a measurable $\sim 5\text{--}10\%$ edge over a matched-capacity MLP on non-linear tasks --- this is a real substrate difference, not a bug; (b) pool averaging compresses the difference rapidly between $N=2$ and $N=32$ but then \emph{plateaus} at $\sim 2\text{--}3\%$ --- a substrate-intrinsic floor that cannot be averaged out; (c) the $5\%$ symmetric-gap contract holds distributionally for every seed at $N \geq 32$, so the paper's substrate-agnostic claim is empirically defensible at realistic pool sizes even though a small, stable $\sim 2\text{--}3\%$ LIF edge exists. The tests \texttt{test\_w2\_hard\_n16\_multiseed\_distribution}, \texttt{test\_w2\_hard\_n32\_multiseed\_closes\_under\_5pct}, \texttt{test\_w2\_hard\_n64\_multiseed\_reaches\_plateau}, and \texttt{test\_w2\_hard\_scaling\_law\_is\_monotonic} pin these claims; Figure~\ref{fig:w2-scaling-law} visualizes them.

\textit{Scale.} All experiments use $N=4$ WMLs with $K=1$--2 fan-out. Whether the 5\% polymorphie gap holds at $N=16$ or $N=32$ is an open question tracked in a separate scaling plan; the router's sparsity and the $\gamma$-priority rule may interact non-trivially at scale.

\textit{Single-seed artefacts.} The Gate P and Gate M pilots in this draft run on a single seed. Where multi-seed data exists (W2, W4, P1), we report it; elsewhere the reader should treat numbers as suggestive rather than statistically validated.

\section{Information Transmission (v0.8)}
\label{sec:info-tx}

The scaling law in \S Threats establishes \textbf{substrate-equivalent task competence}: MLP and LIF cohorts reach comparable accuracies at pool scale, with a $\sim 2\text{--}3\%$ plateau gap. That is Claim A. The title of this paper promises more: \emph{substrate-agnostic information transmission} (Claim B) --- that neuroletters emitted by one substrate can be \emph{decoded coherently} by another. This section reports three direct measurements of Claim B, complemented by a post-hoc mutual-kNN validation in Test~(6) that situates nerve-wml as an empirical instantiation of the Platonic Representation Hypothesis~\cite{huh2024platonic} on heterogeneous biologically-inspired substrates.

\textbf{(1) Mutual information between emitted codes.} For $x$ sampled from \texttt{HardFlowProxyTask}, let $c_{\text{MLP}}(x)$ and $c_{\text{LIF}}(x)$ be the $\arg\max$ codes from each substrate's $\pi$ head. Over five seeds at $N=1$, $\operatorname{MI}(c_{\text{MLP}}, c_{\text{LIF}}) / H(c_{\text{MLP}}) \approx 0.91$. Extended to the $N=16$ pool (64 MLP--LIF cross-pairs per seed over three seeds, 192 pair measurements total), the ratio \emph{strengthens} to $0.96$ --- pool-scale averaging aligns codes across substrates, not just accuracies. Two independently trained substrate populations share $\sim 95\%$ of their code information; they are \emph{not} encoding orthogonally despite different dynamics.

\textbf{(2) Round-trip fidelity.} A two-stage pipeline
$x \to \texttt{MLP.emit} \to T_{M \to L} \to \texttt{LIF.emit} \to T_{L \to M} \to \arg\max$
is trained by fine-tuning only the two transducers $T_{M \to L}, T_{L \to M} \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times 64}$; substrates are frozen. Across three seeds, $\text{acc}_{\text{round-trip}} / \text{acc}_{\text{direct}} = \{0.91, 1.07, 0.98\}$, mean $0.99$. Code information survives the cross-substrate pass virtually intact.

\textbf{(3) Cross-substrate merge.} Freeze a trained MLP and a trained LIF (both their cores and readouts). Train only a transducer $T : \mathbb{R}^{64} \to \mathbb{R}^{n_{\text{neurons}}}$ mapping MLP emissions to LIF input features. Measure LIF task accuracy when its \emph{sole input} is MLP-emitted logits via $T$: the LIF never sees $x$ directly. Across three seeds, merge ratio $\text{acc}_{\text{cross}} / \text{acc}_{\text{MLP}} = \{1.00, 0.96, 0.96\}$, mean $0.97$. A frozen LIF can recover $97\%$ of an MLP's task competence from its emitted codes alone, through a learned linear transducer.

\textbf{(4b) Architecture scale (v1.2).} At $d_\text{hidden}=128$ with the synthetic \texttt{HardFlowProxyTask}, the substrate asymmetry \emph{amplifies} rather than shrinking — $\text{gap} \approx 26\%$ median over 3 seeds. LIF's spike expressivity scales with $n_\text{neurons}$, and on XOR-style tasks this edge widens. Architecture scale and pool scale are orthogonal dimensions. \section{Interpretability}
\label{sec:interp}

Because each WML owns a local 64-code codebook, the learned alphabet
is open to inspection. We build a \emph{semantics table} per WML
(\texttt{interpret.code\_semantics.build\_semantics\_table}) that
records, for each code $c \in \{0, \dots, 63\}$: the top-$K$ input
summaries whose forward pass picks $c$ as the $\pi$-head argmax, the
mean hidden-state centroid over those inputs, the softmax-derived
next-code distribution, and the count of mapped samples. A $50$-line
$k$-means clusterer groups the 64 centroids into 8 semantic buckets;
a plain-HTML report (\texttt{interpret.visualise.render\_html\_report})
renders one row per code with a cluster-coloured dot, centroid norm,
and top-input statistics. \texttt{gate-interp-passed} verifies that
the pipeline completes in under 60\,s, produces a cluster-entropy
above 2 bits (non-degenerate assignments), and writes a report file
of at least 1\,KB. The artefact is a reviewer-inspectable
\texttt{code\,$\to$\,concept} table --- not a universal semantics, but
a reproducible snapshot of what the trained WML treats as equivalent.

\section{Integrations}
\label{sec:integrations}

Beyond the core gates, four integration surfaces are validated as software
pipelines. All are env-gated and return to a no-op when the target is absent,
so nerve-wml can be deployed alongside heterogeneous hosts without coupling
to their release cadences.

\textit{Adaptive alphabet (Gate Adaptive).}
The 64-code codebook is fixed per WML at gate time.
\texttt{track\_p.adaptive\_codebook.AdaptiveCodebook} implements an
\texttt{active\_mask} over a fixed 64-slot storage so shrink and grow
become logical (not physical) operations. \texttt{bridge.transducer\_resize}
produces a reshaped \texttt{Transducer} preserving argmax on kept rows.
\texttt{gate-adaptive-passed} verifies a shrink-then-grow cycle completes
without the alphabet collapsing below 4 codes.

\textit{Neuromorphic export (Gate Neuro).} LifWML $\to$ INT8 artefact
(\texttt{neuromorphic.export.quantize\_lif\_wml}) $\to$ pure-numpy
replay (\texttt{MockNeuromorphicRunner}) $\to$ vendor stubs for
Intel Loihi~2 and BrainChip Akida. On an untrained LifWML the
PyTorch-to-mock delta is $\sim 19\%$ (INT8 quantization of binary-like
codebooks is coarse); trained LIFs are expected to tighten this.
The vendor SDKs are not runtime deps; see
\texttt{docs/neuromorphic/deployment-guide.md}.

\textit{Dream-of-kiki consolidation (Gate Dream, partial).}
\texttt{bridge.dream\_bridge.DreamBridge} collects $\varepsilon$ traces,
encodes them as schema-v0 \texttt{np.int32} arrays of shape
$[n\_\text{events}, 4]$, and applies a consolidation delta (supplied
externally by \texttt{kiki\_oniric}) to per-edge transducers in place.
End-to-end validation ($\gamma$ emission $\to$ $\theta$ collection
$\to$ zero-delta consolidation) is covered by
\texttt{scripts/ablation\_n3\_predictive.py}, confirming the pipeline
plumbing independently of the upstream \texttt{kiki\_oniric} release.
CI uses a \texttt{MockConsolidator} returning zero-delta;
\texttt{gate-dream-passed} is marked \emph{partial} pending the
\texttt{kiki\_oniric} v0.5+ public \texttt{consolidate()} surface.

\textit{LLM advisor for micro-kiki (Gate LLM Advisor).}
\texttt{bridge.kiki\_nerve\_advisor.NerveWmlAdvisor} exposes
\texttt{advise(query\_tokens, current\_route) -> dict | None}.
It is env-gated (\texttt{NERVE\_WML\_ENABLED}) and
checkpoint-path-gated (\texttt{NERVE\_WML\_CHECKPOINT\_PATH}). Warm-path
latency is under 50\,ms on commodity Apple Silicon; disabled-path
overhead is under 1\,ms. The advisor \textbf{never raises} --- every
exception returns \texttt{None}, so the host runtime is not destabilised
by advisor failures. A self-contained downstream-PR recipe is at
\texttt{docs/integration/micro-kiki-wiring.md}.

\textit{Downstream composability (bouba\_sens).}
The v1.4.0--v1.5.0 feature releases (\texttt{plasticity\_schedule} +
\texttt{constellation\_lock\_after} for B-1 Amedi-2007 gap probes,
\texttt{TransducerGating.GUMBEL\_SOFTMAX} for B-2 Me3-delta migration,
and \texttt{MlpWML.from\_spectrogram(\ldots)} for the shared STFT-carrier
path) were filed by \texttt{bouba\_sens}, the first empirical consumer
of the nerve-wml downstream API. All three surfaces are opt-in and
backward-compatible with the v1.2.3 scientific baseline
(\S\ref{sec:info-tx}); \texttt{bouba\_sens} ADR-0007 paired-run CLI
(\texttt{eval --t1-ckpt/--t2-ckpt}) consumes them to produce the
5-world grid. Composability in the other direction (downstream
\emph{changes} to nerve-wml) is enforced by 102 tests plus the
\texttt{gate-*-passed} invariant tags.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
The experiments in this draft use toy signals and small WML pools
($N=4$). Scaling to larger pools, LLM-backed WMLs, and hardware
neuromorphic deployment is left for follow-up work. The $\gamma$
priority rule suffices in simulation; a phase-locked oscillator
regime would give the same invariant without the rule and is a
natural next step.

\textbf{$\gamma/\theta$ empirical stress.} The $\gamma/\theta$ multiplexing
invariant (N-3: $\text{role}{=}\varepsilon \Leftrightarrow \text{phase}{=}\theta$,
\S\ref{sec:method}) is formally specified and unit-tested
(\texttt{tests/unit/test\_invariants.py}), but the experiments in this draft
run the \texttt{HardFlowProxyTask} pipeline with the $\theta$ phase inactive
throughout training --- only the $\gamma$ pathway is stressed. An ablation
of the \texttt{strict\_n3} flag on this pipeline therefore yields
$\Delta = 0$ at four significant figures across $N \in \{16, 32, 64\}$
over 5 seeds (\texttt{scripts/ablation\_n3.py}): there are simply no
$\varepsilon$-letters to validate. Two complementary follow-up experiments confirm this interpretation.
A full $\gamma \to \theta \to$ consolidate loop where MLP WMLs emit
$\varepsilon$ naturally via \texttt{emit\_head\_eps} (surprise $>$ 0.30)
over 3 seeds yields $\Delta = 0$ exactly
(\texttt{scripts/ablation\_n3\_predictive.py}) --- the invariant is
satisfied by construction since WMLs hard-code \texttt{phase=Phase.THETA}
when emitting $\varepsilon$. A violation-injection guard test
(\texttt{scripts/ablation\_n3\_guard.py}) confirms that strict\_n3=True
catches $N \cdot v$ injected violations within binomial noise at
$v \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.25, 0.50\}$ (3 seeds, $N{=}1000$ letters)
while strict\_n3=False lets them pass silently. Together, these three
ablations establish that N-3 is a formal correctness contract --- a
type-checker for the protocol --- rather than a differential mechanism:
measurable impact requires deliberate violation injection rather than
emerging from a correct pipeline. This does not invalidate the
Bastos-Friston 2012 ancestry, since biological $\gamma / \theta$
multiplexing is also a correctness-enforcing constraint, not a free
parameter that tunes computational behaviour.

Wiring the adaptive-alphabet operations into live WMLs at training
